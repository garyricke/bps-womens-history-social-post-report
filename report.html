The BPS101 Women's History Month Controversy

Executive Overview

The Post That Sparked a National Debate

On the morning of Thursday, March 12, 2026, the Batavia School District 101 (BPS101) communications department posted a Women's History Month tribute to U.S. Representative Sarah McBride on its social media channels. Within hours, the post was absorbed into a national ideological apparatus — triggering demands for firings, board resignations, and an institutional communication overhaul.

The post featured a biographical sketch of Rep. McBride — noting her birth in Wilmington, Delaware, her tenure as national press secretary for the Human Rights Campaign, her work passing non-discrimination laws in the Delaware state legislature, and her historic election as the first openly transgender person elected to the United States Congress on November 5, 2024.


BPS101's stated mission for its social media presence was to "educate parents" and celebrate "news and celebrations" of the district community. The district's own Communications Guidelines call for posts 2–3 times per week on Facebook and characterize the district's digital platforms as tools for "sharing important announcements, upcoming events, and news stories."


ℹ️

What was in the post? The post described McBride as a "groundbreaking" figure whose work was "instrumental" in passing non-discrimination laws. It included an image and biographical detail about her career in advocacy and elected office. Critics called it "glowing" and "jubilant," with what was described as "a childishly enthusiastic exclamation point."

Chronology of Events

How It Unfolded

From the moment the post went live to the board's formal response, the controversy moved with remarkable speed across platforms and geographic boundaries.

Conservative outlet Breakthrough Ideas publishes a lengthy analysis labeling BPS101's post as an endorsement of "trans superstition" that "denies the meaning of physical embodiment," potentially jeopardizing federal funding under Trump's Executive Order.

Conservative outlets including the Daily Caller and national platforms associated with Tucker Carlson amplify the story. Screenshots circulate on pages across the country before deletion — making retraction effectively impossible.

March 12–15, 2026

BPS101 removes the post following legal consultation. Communications Director Holly Deitchman cites that the post "no longer served its educational purpose" once it became a magnet for vitriol. A new policy is announced: all comments disabled on future social media posts.

The first issue of BPS101's new print newsletter, "The District Dispatch," is mailed to all residences in the community — a deliberate pivot from volatile social media to controlled, one-way print communication.

The Mental Health Argument — Expanded

Heather Delmaso: "Real Kids, Not Political Talking Points" Support▼

Delmaso's full testimony centered on the statistical reality of LGBTQ youth in the district. She cited that roughly 9 to 10% of teenagers identify as LGBTQ, meaning in any school, on any team, or in any club, there are students who fall into that group. She emphasized that these youth experience significantly higher rates of depression and suicide attempts, and that research consistently shows one of the strongest protective factors is "feeling accepted and safe in the communities around them."

Her argument was that for some students, "simply seeing that their school recognizes that reality can help them feel safe and accepted and know they belong here too." She concluded: "They are not political talking points. They are real kids in this district. They participate in sports, perform in band and theater, belong to clubs and honor societies."

The 40% Statistic: NIH & Trevor Project Research Data▼

A speaker at the meeting cited specific research: "I think of preventing and responding to bullying, using the correct pronouns — I think of that as suicide prevention. Research from the National Institutes of Health and the Trevor Project demonstrates that for trans and non-binary youth who have affirming adults and spaces in their lives, they have 40% less risk of attempting suicide compared with their unsupported trans peers."

This statistic reframed the controversy from a debate about "wokeism" to a matter of public health and institutional responsibility. The speaker argued that the post was not a political statement but a safety intervention, and that its deletion amounted to "capitulating to bullies."

Richard Den: The Opposition Case Opposed▼

Richard Den was the sole speaker in opposition. His testimony contained several points of critique: he demanded the immediate firing of the Communications Director (referring to her as "Miss Dipman"), asked all board members to resign, and questioned how "in a town of 27,000 people you can't find one female to celebrate." He also argued the post contained factual errors — calling McBride a "senator" when she is a Representative — and claimed the post "negatively impacted the self-esteem of real girls within this school district."

When he attempted to ask the board directly whether they "support teaching children that men can become females," the Board President replied: "Not at all." (meaning not during public comment). His request for a direct answer was declined per board procedures.

⚡

Den also cited Trump's Executive Order "Ending Radical Indoctrination in K-12 Schooling," suggesting the post may have jeopardized federal funding — an argument also made extensively in conservative media coverage.

Ryan Hatfield: The Civics Argument Support▼

Hatfield presented what the analysis calls the "Civics Narrative" — celebrating McBride not as an ideological statement but as recognition of a historic democratic achievement. He noted McBride won the November 2024 general election with 58% of the vote, becoming the first openly transgender member of the US Congress on January 3, 2025.

His argument: "The post and the inclusion of Sarah doesn't take away from but rather enhances the broader picture of the contributions and achievement of all women. It showcases the diversity of women and helps paint a complete picture."

The "Ontological Critique" — Breakthrough Ideas' Philosophical Framing Conservative Media▼

Breakthrough Ideas moved beyond political disagreement into what the analysis calls "ontological dispute." The outlet characterized the district's post as endorsing a "trans superstition" that "denies the meaning of physical embodiment" — using the phrase "Gnostic superstition" to describe gender identity and the imagery of "a gendered ghost inside the wrong machine."

This rhetorical strategy serves to move the debate away from civil rights and into a fundamental conflict over the nature of reality. By framing gender dysphoria as something with "no proof," the outlet delegitimizes not just McBride's recognition but the entire conceptual framework of transgender identity.

The outlet also linked the post to Trump's Executive Order "Ending Radical Indoctrination in K-12 Schooling", suggesting BPS101 had potentially jeopardized federal funding — converting an administrative choice into a financial risk argument for taxpayers.

Institutional Response

How BPS101 Changed After the Controversy

The controversy triggered a fundamental rethinking of how the district communicates with its community — a strategic retreat from interactive social media toward controlled, one-way channels.

The Deletion Decision

Communications Director Holly Deitchman stated the post was deleted based on legal advice and that it "no longer served its educational purpose" once it became a magnet for vitriol. The district made clear the removal was to protect students from further harm, not to capitulate to the criticism of the post's content.


🔒

New Policy: "All comments will be turned off on future social media posts." This represents a strategic shift from an interactive public forum model to a broadcast-only model.


Supporters at the meeting were disappointed. Several speakers urged the district to develop a "plan to handle online hostility" rather than resorting to deletion and silence. One community member argued the district was setting "a poor example" for the very students it is tasked with protecting.


⚠️

The Dunning Problem: A Facebook commenter noted that before deletion, "multiple conservative groups from around the country took screenshots... It's still circulating, still being shared, still sparking disgusting, hateful comments from a widespread audience." Deletion was effectively too late.

New Communication Initiatives

📰 The District Dispatch (Print Newsletter) ▼

A new print newsletter mailed to all residences in the community, twice per year. The first issue was mailed March 20, 2026 — just days after the controversy peaked. It featured a story on the first-grade team at Grace McWain and a letter from the Superintendent. This deliberately bypasses social media algorithms that favor outrage and rage-clicks.

🎙️ "Kids These Days" Podcast ▼

An intergenerational conversation podcast where older community members and high school students discuss shared topics. The first episode was recorded two weeks before the March 16 meeting, featuring a BHS senior student and Craig Foltos, a local barbershop owner. The aim: give older residents who say "kids these days" a real foundation for that conversation with actual students.

🎓 Student Board Member Program ▼

The board moved an action item to invite Batavia High School students to apply for roles as Student Representatives, providing an advisory role, sharing student perspectives, and "amplifying the voices of younger students" in board governance. This directly responds to criticism that the district was making decisions about students without adequately involving them.

📚 Book Vending Machine at Hooverwood ▼

While not a direct response to the controversy, Principal Sean Diamond's book vending machine at Hooverwood Elementary — celebrated at the same March 16 meeting — was highlighted by analysts as a deliberate "positive counter-narrative." The machine holds 300 books, has distributed approximately 40 books in its first month, and features "award-winning, diverse books" curated by the principal. Students earn books through reading challenges, behavior programs, and classroom activities.

The Failed Referendum Context

Previous referendums to replace aging buildings like H.C. Storm and Louise White had failed twice — largely due to voter opposition to tax increases. The failed $140 million referendum forced the district to renovate rather than rebuild, avoiding "sunk costs" but leaving the infrastructure crisis unresolved.


Because voters rejected tax increases, BPS101 is now seeking state approval for HLS bonds — a mechanism that bypasses the referendum process for health and life safety projects specifically. One board member noted the community "probably aren't going to like the $155 a year or whatever it works out as, but people will like it even less if their kids are in school and something terrible happens."

The ideological noise was real — but so was the physical reality competing for the board's attention.

Conclusion

The Media as a Catalyst for Institutional Evolution

The BPS101 controversy illustrates the high stakes of institutional communication in 2026. A single social media post — intended to celebrate Women's History Month — became a case study in how local decisions are consumed by national ideological machinery.

The "40% reduction in suicide risk" statistic stands as the central pillar of the district's defense, transforming the debate from one about "superstition" to one about "safety." The 12-to-1 ratio at the board meeting reveals a community that, in its physical form, remained largely supportive of inclusive goals — even as digital platforms amplified a hateful minority.

Ultimately, BPS101's response — deleting the post, disabling comments, launching print newsletters and podcasts — represents the emerging standard for public institutions navigating the "architecture of outrage." Whether this represents wise crisis management or a surrender to intimidation remains the defining question the community continues to debate.

The Three Unanswered Questions

1. Should BPS101 have anticipated the national amplification, and does anticipating backlash mean accepting self-censorship?

2. Does disabling comments "protect students" or does it silence the community dialogue that support and affirmation actually require?

3. With $17.6M+ in failing infrastructure and a community polarized by a deleted post, where should BPS101's leadership direct its finite energy and political capital?

Deep Dive: Who Is Sarah McBride?

Biographical Facts

  • Born in Wilmington, Delaware
  • Former National Press Secretary for the Human Rights Campaign (HRC)
  • Served in the Delaware State Senate — instrumental in passing the Healthy Delaware Families Act, which provided paid family and medical leave
  • Expanded school-based mental health programs during her state legislative tenure
  • Won the November 2024 general election with 58% of the vote
  • Sworn in as the first openly transgender member of the United States Congress on January 3, 2025

Endorsements

McBride was endorsed by the National Education Association (NEA) for her congressional run. She also received endorsements from Sen. John Fetterman and other Democratic figures.

Why BPS101 Featured Her

The district characterized McBride as "groundbreaking" and her work as "instrumental" in passing non-discrimination laws. Her historic election as the first transgender Congressperson made her a natural choice for a Women's History Month post celebrating political firsts and representation.

The Criticism

Critics argued: (1) McBride has no ties to Batavia; (2) her "personal history has mostly been lived as a boy and man" (Holly Miller, Facebook); (3) a Women's History Month post should feature "a biological woman." The most extreme critics used dehumanizing language, calling her a "crossdressing man" and a "biological male."

Facebook commenter Stefin Steberl noted: "Saying someone 'lived as a boy or man' isn't how many transgender people experience their lives. It reflects how others may have perceived them, not who they are."

Global Context: IWD Website Controversy

The Guardian Report (March 15, 2026)

The Guardian reported on a significant parallel controversy: the website internationalwomensday.com, which major UK brands including Sainsbury's and Barclays had mistakenly treated as an official UN-linked site, was condemned by campaigners for "exploiting a social movement for financial gain."

The UN's Position

The United Nations formally distanced itself from the site, which campaigners argue is "watering down" International Women's Day into "almost meaningless marketing" where commercial themes don't align with a "genuine desire to advance women's rights."

Relevance to Batavia

This global context matters because when BPS101 chose McBride for Women's History Month, it was operating in an environment where the very definition and ownership of "women's history" was actively contested worldwide — not just locally. The critics' argument that the district chose a "man who pretends to be a woman" to represent women's history was framed by some outlets as a symptom of this broader "watering down" phenomenon.
